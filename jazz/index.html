principles

How to use it

Six rules that keep it unmistakably Jazz — soft, scattered, and round, with one loud squiggle holding it together.

  • Pastel, not neonKeep fills soft and chalky. Let the two-stroke squiggle be the loudest thing on the page — save the Pop set for accents and the Loud theme.
  • Two strokes, always crossingThe teal goes under, the grape crosses over. One squiggle alone isn't Jazz; the crossing is the signature.
  • Scatter the confettiToss dots, triangles, and squiggles at irregular intervals and jaunty angles. Never line them up in a tidy grid.
  • Round everythingPill buttons, soft corners, rounded type. The 90s cup had no sharp edges — neither should you.
  • Outline plus offsetA thick ink outline and a hard, blur-free shadow turn every element into a peel-off sticker. No soft shadows, no gradients.
  • Off-white is the cupLet the paper-white ground breathe and keep most of the page calm, so the pattern reads as a print floating on a cup.
accessibility

Accessibility

Pastels are pretty and low-contrast — the honest tradeoff of the look. The system leans on ink outlines and dark text to stay legible.

Built in

Body text is near-black --ink on --paper, clearing WCAG AA · every element carries a thick ink outline, so shape and edge never depend on color alone · a fat :focus-visible ring sits on buttons, swatches, tabs, and the switch · the switch is a real checkbox · floats and the bobbing cup freeze under prefers-reduced-motion.

Your part

Never set body text in a pastel on white — reserve pastels for fills, the outline gives them definition · keep label words on badges; don't signal status by hue alone · on a teal/grape/berry fill, use white or --ink text and re-check contrast · Loud is for emphasis, not for walls of text.

origin

Heritage

The pattern is real, and it's everywhere. Designed by Gina Ekiss for the Sweetheart Cup Company around 1991, the "Jazz" design won an internal contest and went on to wrap millions of disposable cups — the unofficial wallpaper of the American 90s.

c. 1991
The Jazz cup
Gina Ekiss designs the teal-and-purple squiggle for Sweetheart Cup Co. It wins a staff contest and ships on disposable cups nationwide.
early 90s
Memphis, softened
The Memphis Group's postmodern geometry trickles down into mass culture — Trapper Keepers, Saved by the Bell titles, mall carpets — in friendlier pastels.
mid 90s
Peak cup
Dentist offices, water coolers, vending machines, birthday parties. The squiggle becomes so common it turns invisible — then, decades on, iconic.
