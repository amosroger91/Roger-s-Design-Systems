Glass interface language · edition 02

Aurora

A glass interface language of translucent, light-bending chrome over flowing color. Surfaces you can see through, lit with a wet gloss and a tint that's yours — descended from Windows Vista and the Aero era, re-tokenized, hardened, and made graceful enough to ship today.

Luna / XP 2001 › Windows Aero 2006–2007 › Frutiger Aero › the flat years › resurfacing now. Authentic, Energetic, Reflective, Open.

Getting started

Three switches theme the whole language from one place. Aurora is namespaced (--au-* tokens, .glass / component classes), ships an opaque fallback, and degrades gracefully where backdrop-filter is unsupported — so it drops in without collisions.

1 · Set the three theming attributes on <body> (or any subtree).   2 · Use the .glass material and components.   3 · Reference --au-* tokens in your own CSS.

Add .au-reduce-transparency to any scope to make glass opaque, or data-au-theme="light" for the Frutiger bright variant. The OS-level prefers-reduced-transparency and prefers-reduced-motion are honored automatically.

Buttons & controls

Glossy Aero buttons with a bright top bevel and a glow on hover. The solid carries the accent gradient; everything else is glass or quiet. Plus the toggle switch, checkbox, radio, segmented control, slider, and badges — every part keyboard-operable and tint-aware.

Forms & choices

Sunken Aero fields with an inset shadow and an accent focus ring that blooms. Inputs, selects, a slider, and the radio group — all on the active tint.

Tabs & menus

The Aero property-sheet tabs (roving-tabindex, arrow-navigable), a frosted dropdown menu with keyboard support, a breadcrumb, and an accordion. The menu and tooltip are glass too, and fall back to opaque under reduce-transparency.

Status & feedback

Badges pair a colored dot with a label so meaning survives any color-vision difference. Plus the striped progress bar, a spinner, a glass tooltip, and overlays — a toast, a tray balloon, and a focus-trapping modal dialog.

Lists & tables

A sortable, selectable data table with the active-row accent bar — the kind of dense, scannable surface Explorer was built on. Click a header to sort, a row to select.

The desktop

The material assembled into the real thing: a full Explorer window with a frosted title bar, a breadcrumb address bar, a command bar, a navigation tree, and a status bar — plus floating gadgets and a glass taskbar anchored by the pearl. Hover a taskbar item for an Aero Peek thumbnail. Everything here is built from the tokens above and respects the reduce-transparency switch.

Principles

Six rules, each lifted from something Aero actually did — then stated as a rule you can hold a design against.

01

Glass over solid

Chrome is translucent; the world shows through it.

Surfaces are frosted, not painted. Blurring the context behind a pane keeps the user oriented while pushing it back in space.

02

Light is wet

Every surface has a gloss and a specular edge.

A highlight across the top half, a bright top border, and a faint frost grain make the material read as polished glass.

03

Depth through blur

Blur is hierarchy. Sharper is nearer.

Focus comes forward and sharpens; everything else softens and recedes. The amount of blur tells you the layer order.

04

Tint is yours

One accent colors the whole system's glass.

Personalization, not wayfinding. Pick a tint and every pane, control, and glow inherits it — the interface becomes the user's.

05

Glow is feedback

Controls answer with a soft bloom of light.

Hover, focus, and active states bloom rather than snap. The response is warm and physical, never a hard flip.

06

Glass is optional

The whole system survives without transparency.

Reduce transparency, no backdrop-filter, or a low-end GPU all degrade to clean opaque surfaces. Beauty is never load-bearing.

Do, and do not

Do

  • Let glass be translucent — blur real context behind every pane.
  • Give each surface a gloss, a bright specular edge, and a touch of frost grain.
  • Use blur amount to signal layer order: sharper is nearer.
  • Drive the whole system from one accent tint the user picks.
  • Answer interaction with a soft glow that blooms, not a hard flip.
  • Always ship the opaque fallback — test the whole app with glass off.

Do not

  • Fake glass with a flat semi-opaque fill — without blur it's just a tint.
  • Stack many heavy blurs at once; legibility and performance both suffer.
  • Use the tint for wayfinding — it's personalization, so it can't mean "place."
  • Put low-contrast text on raw frosted glass; add a scrim or inner shadow first.
  • Pile on gloss until controls look wet and unreadable.
  • Let the design break when transparency is unavailable or switched off.

Accessibility

Glass is gorgeous and easy to get wrong. Aurora carries its weight here: contrast survives the blur, the whole language has an opaque mode, motion is optional, and every control is reachable and operable from the keyboard.

Built in

The top bar's solid switch sets .au-reduce-transparency, swapping every pane for an opaque surface and dropping the grain — and the OS-level prefers-reduced-transparency is honored automatically. Where backdrop-filter is unsupported, an @supports fallback paints solid surfaces instead of failing to transparent. Accent-on-night text meets contrast at body sizes, and every interactive part shows a 2px accent :focus-visible ring.

Your part

Flip 3D, tabs, menus, and the accordion are real focusable widgets — arrows, Space, Enter, and Esc all behave, and the dialog traps focus and restores it on close. Everything respects prefers-reduced-motion: the aurora and stars stop, flips become instant, the spinner settles. The glass chime is off-able and never plays without a gesture. Keep the layout and contrast off the blur — reserve the tint for chrome and emphasis, and never place body text on raw frosted glass.

Heritage

Windows Vista's Aero was the first mainstream interface composited entirely on the GPU: every window's title bar became real-time frosted glass, blurring and tinting whatever sat behind it. It was glossy, optimistic, and a little utopian — the centerpiece of the broader Frutiger Aero look of bubbles, water, clean skies, and humanist type. The industry abandoned it for flat design a few years later; the instincts — translucency as hierarchy, glass as material, light as feedback — are exactly what Acrylic, vibrancy, and Liquid Glass reach for now.

Plastic, candy-colored, opaque. Friendly but flat — the warm-up for what composition could do.
2006

DWM composites everything. Live frosted glass, the Start pearl, Flip 3D, gadgets, soft glows.
2009
Aero Peek (7)
The glass matured: live thumbnails, Peek-to-desktop, snapping. Aero at its most refined.
2012
The flat years
Metro and the industry strip the glass away. Depth and gloss declared decadent, removed wholesale.
Now
Aurora
The glass returns, re-tokenized: translucency you can theme, with the gloss and optimism intact — and a graceful way to turn it off.
